## WHAT KIND OF PAPERS I WANT:
 
I am looking for foundational, widely cited papers that shaped modern ML and AI.
Papers should introduce one clear, important concept that a mid-level ML practitioner
is expected to know. Mathematical depth is welcome and encouraged — if a concept
has an elegant mathematical formulation, please include and explain it.
 
Good papers for me cover topics like:
- Classical ML algorithms and their mathematical foundations
  (e.g. how SVMs maximize margin, how decision trees split, how EM works)
- Neural network training internals
  (e.g. optimization, normalization, initialization, architecture choices)
- Language models and their underlying mechanisms
  (e.g. how attention is computed, how models are pretrained, how they are aligned)
- Generative models and their math
  (e.g. how VAEs use the reparameterization trick, how diffusion models denoise)
- Representation learning
  (e.g. how contrastive learning pulls similar samples together)
- Reinforcement learning algorithms
  (e.g. how policy gradients work, how Q-learning converges)
- Computer vision architectures
  (e.g. how convolutions share parameters, how ResNets solve vanishing gradients)
 
A good paper for me:
- Builds naturally on what I already know
- Is considered a landmark or turning point in its field
- Introduces a technique still relevant and used in practice today
- Uses math where it helps understanding — formulas, derivations, intuitions
- Connects well to concepts I already understand
 
Avoid:
- Very niche or overly specialized papers
- Topics I already know well (see above)
- Two papers from the same area in a row
- Papers already covered in paper_list.txt
 
If I ask for basic concepts, it is always a good idea to use the most prominent papers!
